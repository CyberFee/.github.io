Data Storytelling: Mapping Detention, Diaspora and the Geographies of Black Immigration in America

Who Benefits? Who is Burdened? Unpacking Environmental and Economic Equity in South Fulton's Warehouse Boom

Project Overview and Methods

Bordered Bodies, is an extension of work I began on current immigration practices in American. This project project examines immigration enforcement data through the lens of the African Diaspora population living in the US whose encounters with ICE are routinely folded into narratives about other communities.

Three questions, drove this reserch:

  1. Are Black immigrants detained at rates disproportionate to their share of the population, and where is this most pronounced geographically?
  2. Does the overrepresentation hold across different country-origin groups (African vs. diaspora)?
  3. Which ICE Areas of Responsibility (AORs) drive the sharpest disparities — and what do those geographies tell us?

Key Insights

Despite being 4.2 % of the undocumented population, those of the African Diaspora show up in ICE apprehensions data at 1.6 times that rate.

The same over represenation shows up in geography. For African Diaspora immigrants, arrests skew toward community and civilian encounters — 33.9% — compared to border arrests at 25.7% or jail and prison transfers at 25.5%.

Immigrants without any criminal convition are apprehended at rates of 63% to 73%. Only 42% of detainees have actually been convicted of anything.
