# Linguistic Justice in Language Technologies

## Introduction

Language is central to the conceptualization of the world and how individuals and groups interpret and engage in that space. Language helps set social realities and also serves to reinforce and maintain cultural knowledge and heritage. However, in the continuously advancing world of AI technology, machine learning (ML) and large language models (LLMs) are rapidly changing the way in which language and language technologies are used. These algorithms and models have expanded AI technologies into diverse fields and coupled with natural language processing (NLP), which use ML to understand and communicate with human language, has led to advances in voice-activated digital assistants like Siri, Alexa and Cortana, translation apps, and chatbots. Autocomplete, autocorrect, survey and sentiment analytics are also other ways in which this technology has been used. However, these prompt-based tools and analytics techniques are largely developed on standard forms of English and a small group of other languages; or they are trained on an un-curated Internet based dataset which is primarily written in English (Nee, Macfarlane Smith, Sheares, & Rustagi, 2021). This is problematic when estimates indicate there are over 7,000 languages in use today (Eberhard, Simons, & Fennig, 2024), but the primary training language is limited to a select few. This algorithm modeling not only ignores the global majority and other specific populations and languages, but also places restrictions on the ability of historically marginalized communities and groups to fully utilize current and developing technologies. These data limitations increase the digital language divide potentially widening social and economic gaps and creating questions of distributive justice, fairness, equity, and the allocation of resources (Jost & Kay, 2010). The mining of big data must embrace cultural perspectives and evaluate how data are "generated, curated, and how they permeate and exert power…" (Iliadis & Russo, 2016). This is then an issue of cultural justice which emphasizes the importance of recognition and respect for diverse cultural identities to counter cultural domination and non-recognition (Gerwitz & Cribb, 2002).

## Power Dynamics, Language and Machine Learning 

Language being used as a means of asserting power over another group and as a tool of oppression span history. Colonialism proliferated English within the British colonies, which were comprised of a diverse mix of Europeans, suppressed and contributed to the decline of Native American languages, and prohibited the linguistic heritage of enslaved Africans. For African Americans, even following emancipation, anti-literacy laws further asserted the existing power structures. Other examples include banning of native African languages and the imposition of Afrikaans in apartheid South Africa and the suppression of minority languages, including Yiddish, by Nazi Germany. In the last decade and half, some states have enacted policies that limit or attempt to end bilingual education for native Spanish speakers. (Arizona State Senate, n.d.) (Ballotopedia, n.d.). These historical examples reinforce the importance of not replicating similar linguistic frameworks or power dynamics within natural language processes and generative AI systems (Nee, Macfarlane, Sheares, & Rustagi, 2021).

Present day, the dominant languages, or languages with the most speakers across the world, are English, Mandarin, Hindi and Spanish. See Figure 1 (Eberhard, Simons & Fennig, 2024). Considered well-sourced or high-resource languages because of the large amount of digital data readily available, these languages typically have long written traditions, preserved text and a current presence in popular media on which to train language-based systems. Under-served or so-called low resource languages may have a more oral tradition and thus less recorded historical references.

In addition, communities of speakers of low-resourced languages may have limited access to technology and significantly smaller digital footprints, creating a system of non-representation and often misrepresentation in NLP and LLMs. For example, the standard versions of English dominate generative AI and NLP. In contrast, colloquial forms of English that reflect diverse cultural identities such as African American Vernacular English (AAVE), Jamaican Patois or the Creole languages of the south, including Louisiana Creole French and the Gullah or Geechee found in South Carolina and Georgia, are only recently being sourced through such open communities as Creole NLP (https://creole-nlp.github.io/). AmericansNLP is fostering research to include the indigenous languages of the Americas into machine learning paradigms (https://turing.iimas.unam.mx/americasnlp/). From a global perspective, other indigenous languages and several African languages are still missing or underrepresented in training models but grassroot efforts are being made to change this (Mohamed, Benelallam, Allak, & Gaanoun, 2024) (Ògúnrẹ̀mí, Nekoto & Samuel, 2023) (Cooper, Heldreth, & Hutchinson, 2024).

## Social Justice and the Decolonization of LLMs and NLP

The underrepresentation of the global majority within language data used in machine learning has far reaching implications in terms of equitable access to the benefits within an ever-expanding world of technology. When testing generative AI language models' accuracy rates are likely not emulated when using languages other than English. These may limit the understanding of complex topics, include biased or stereotyped language or miss nuanced contexts of cultural significance (Hu et al., 2023). Linguistic biases within AI assisted mathematical problem solving have also been observed (Jun, 2023). These bias leave speakers of non-dominant languages with limited access to AI-powered educational tools and resources; so a student who speaks a less common dialect may struggle to use AI powered tutoring systems or language learning apps, hindering their educational progress. Generative AI tools used to screen resumes may also inadvertently discriminate against candidates from marginalized backgrounds due to limited language diversity in the training data. NLP systems used in healthcare might not accurately interpret symptoms described using culturally specific language or expressions leading to a patient receiving a less accurate diagnosis when a machine learning algorithm is employed. These are only a few examples of the limitation present in LLMs and NLP technologies that can exacerbate exiting inequities and potentially create new ones. Addressing these issues require directed efforts to make the language models, the processing systems and data sources more inclusive, diverse and representative. This mitigation might include development of multilingual and dialect-inclusive models, inclusion of diverse populations and population data in the algorithmic training data, or creating specific and focused NLP tools for low resource languages or dialects (Cooper, Heldreth & Hutchinson, 2024).

Under-served versions of dominant languages, Indigenous, regional, and languages with more oral traditions have to be prioritized in order to shift the current data, knowledge and power gaps that continue to perpetuate historical, social and economic injustices. Researchers, programmers and industries must critically examine whose interest are served, what those interests are and to what extent the technology either supports or hinders these interests. This social justice framework moves away from a patriarchal view that assumes all communities want and need the same generative technologies. An intentional shift from the default whiteness of technological development and the prioritizing of efficiency over equity is required to move towards a just social tech system in which the allocation of tech resources and the use of these tools are equitably distributed (Benjamin,2019 p. 98) (Jost & Kay, 2010).

## NOTES:

### Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Large Language Models (LLMs)

Natural language processing (NLP) and large language models (LLMs) are both technologies that use human language to enable human-like interactions with machines, but they differ in their approach and capabilities:

**NLP**

Focuses on understanding human language and structures. NLP uses algorithmic modeling and defined rules to analyze and manipulate language. NLP is good at specialized tasks like syntax parsing and entity recognition, and is more efficient for executing specific tasks. NLP is used in applications like processing medical records, sentiment analysis, and customer service.

**LLMs**

Focuses on generating text based on statistical patterns learned from data. LLMs are trained on large amounts of data to predict the next word or phrase in a sequence. LLMs are good at generating human-like text and managing a wide range of language tasks. LLMs are used in applications like creating financial reports, conducting market analyses, and automating customer service interactions. 

NLP and LLMs can complement each other, and new techniques are being developed that combine their strengths. For example, LLMs can be used to streamline the building and training of chatbots, and NLP can be used to make chatbots subject matter experts. 

### Machine Learning (ML), Generative AI, and Natural Language Processing (NLP)

Machine learning (ML), generative AI, and natural language processing (NLP) are all related to artificial intelligence (AI), but they have different purposes and capabilities: 

**Machine learning (ML)**

ML is used to understand and predict data. For example, in autonomous vehicles, ML algorithms can analyze sensor data to help the car drive in real time. 

**Generative AI**

Generative AI creates new data, such as text, images, audio, or video, based on input data. For example, generative AI can create product descriptions, recommendations, and promotions, or update product displays based on sales data. 

**Natural language processing (NLP)**

NLP is used to understand and interact with human language. NLP systems analyze data and make predictions, such as fraud detection or sentiment analysis. NLP systems can also perform tasks like translation and summarization. 

NLP is a subset of AI, and both NLP and ML are subsets of AI. In fact, ML algorithms were incorporated into NLP in the 2000s, which led to the development of more complex NLP models. 

### NLP Disambiguation

NLP can refer to either natural language processing or neuro-linguistic programming: 

**Natural language processing (NLP)**

A branch of artificial intelligence (AI) that uses machine learning to enable computers to understand and communicate with human language. NLP is used in many everyday products and services, including: 

- Voice-activated digital assistants like Siri, Alexa, and Cortana 
- Email-scanning programs to identify spam 
- Translation apps 
- Search engines 
- Chatbots for customer service 
- Voice-operated GPS systems
